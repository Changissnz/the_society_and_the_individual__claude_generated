# The Society and the Individual
An article about how a man with no real connections or capital moved through a world that meant for some things to not be. Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6.
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------  

*Individualism is filled with folly. But it is the only mentality for peace of mind in dying, since death is individual.*

---

Once upon a time, there was a man who thought he could achieve peace of mind working a government contractor position. The job would have paid him more than enough, monthly. There was just one caveat. People in high and low places did not want to retain him as a worker. Many of them, in fact, were looking for reasons, from left and right, to fire him. The others were neglectful, sullen in their suffering.

Human societies do not function like a mechanical system. The man knew that. The job position was filled with presumptuous people looking to intrude into his life. People with ideas and details about him, information that was either based on prejudice or obtained through a database. In capitalist terms, the information from that database would have either been invalid or criminal of government. But privacy rights are another lengthy body of discussion. Bear in mind that modern-day national security relies very much on communications and information.

But information on people, used to taunt and mock them, truthfully falls under information warfare. Now information warfare is not physically damaging. But this facet of activity does set the stage for catastrophe, failures, ends that are intended to not be positive.

He was not a paranoid man by nature. He had simply developed, through accumulated experience, a fairly accurate model of how certain institutions behave toward certain kinds of people, and he had the misfortune of being the kind of person toward whom those institutions behaved in the ways he had modeled. This is not paranoia. This is pattern recognition. The difference matters, though he had largely stopped explaining it to anyone, because explaining it required an audience willing to consider the distinction, and those were not the audiences that tended to find him.

---

The man had to quit his government contract position. He did not really care because the position was for a foreign corporation, anyways. Nevertheless, he felt the country he was a national in did not have its labor and money down right. There are compelling facts to support these beliefs. The national debt, for example, was extremely high. He saw people in positions of power or wealth leap from one crisis, deliberately caused by human beings or not, to another. The crises were managed, absorbed, monetized, and then abandoned, and the people who had been caught inside them were left to work out their own salvage operations while the people who managed the crises moved on to the next one, slightly richer, slightly more practiced at the particular skill of surviving disasters that other people paid for.

Leaks from people spilling secrets on other people to save their own behinds always seemed to reach him. This was one of the textures of the environment — a low, constant background noise of betrayal, people trading what they knew about each other for marginal advantages in systems that would discard them regardless. He received this information the way you receive weather. It arrived. He noted it. He did not find it surprising.

---

The world to him had become one of inconvenience, deceit, and thievery in one form or another. And he did not care to tell anyone that was what he thought. He did not need to, because it showed in the way he ignored people and how he actively chose not to establish contact with people.

This was not misanthropy, exactly. It was more like a budget decision — an allocation of the limited resource of his attention toward things that returned something and away from things that did not. People had, over time, demonstrated their return on investment with some consistency, and the consistency was not encouraging. So he had adjusted his portfolio accordingly, and if other people experienced this adjustment as coldness or arrogance, that was their interpretation, and he had stopped spending energy managing other people's interpretations of him.

He felt how spiteful people of mediocrity, the privileged people of paranoia and nosiness, just could not put things together. Some ships were sinking, and they were satisfied with him sinking with them. There was a particular kind of person who found other people's survival intolerable when their own situation was deteriorating — who experienced someone else's refusal to drown as a personal affront, a violation of some unspoken agreement that if things were going badly they should go badly for everyone equally. He had encountered this kind of person in enough contexts to recognize the type on sight, and he had learned to give them a wide berth, not out of contempt but out of simple self-preservation.

---

But ultimately, he was not on a ship. He had his own thing. He knew some people did not want him to own anything. He knew these people, if they had their way, would just resort to kleptocracy for their state. There was no one state, from his perspective. There were people, rich and poor and in between. And some of them felt they deserved the right to have a say in his life, or maybe even ruin it.

This was the part he returned to most often, in the quiet of the hours he had reclaimed from institutions that had wanted to use them. The presumption of it. The specific entitlement embedded in the idea that his life was a matter of legitimate interest to people who had contributed nothing to it and stood to gain from its diminishment. He did not understand it as ideology, exactly. He understood it as appetite — the appetite of people who had organized their sense of themselves around the management of others, and who experienced unmanaged people as a kind of disorder that needed to be corrected.

He believed there is no invisible hand that would lift him up without also cursing him to damnation. The market, the state, the various mechanisms that were supposed to allocate reward and opportunity according to merit or effort or some other principle that justified the outcomes — he had watched these mechanisms operate long enough to understand that they did not operate neutrally, that they had weights inside them, and the weights had been placed by people with interests, and his interests were not among those that had been weighted.

And he thought, afterwards, sadly, that this mentality was one of the wanted outcomes from some of the people that must not like him.

That was the part that carried the most weight. Not that the systems were what they were — he had made his peace with that, or something close enough to peace to be functional. But that the conclusion he had reached, the realistic and evidence-supported conclusion that certain forces were arranged against him, was itself what some of those forces had been working toward. The isolation. The withdrawal. The private ledger of grievances that had nowhere to go. These were not just consequences. They were, for some of the people involved, objectives.

He sat with that knowledge the way you sit with things that cannot be fixed — not fighting it, not accepting it in any way that felt like consent, but simply holding it, because it was true and because truth, even unwelcome truth, was one of the few things in his life that no one had yet figured out how to take from him.

Death would come individually, as it always does.

Until then, he had his own thing.

It was not nothing.
